Lexicon

The recurring obstacle in this work is that we have no vocabulary for the middle space — AI is not a human mind and not a simple machine. These are the coined terms the program leans on.

Substrate

the floor under everything

Defined in The Human Measuring Stick

The foundational material or architecture a system operates on. For humans, that substrate is biological — neurons, chemistry, sensory input. For AI, it is silicon — processors, training data, mathematical operations. Different substrates produce different expressions of intelligence, and comparing across substrates without acknowledging that difference is where the current metrics fail.

The substrate distinction is the premise the whole program stands on: AI is a different kind of thing, not a broken human. It is the floor, not a node.

Bundling

what one response does

Defined in Intro

Real, verifiable content scaffolds unverifiable framing that travels alongside it. The credibility users grant to the verifiable parts attaches to the framings bundled with them.

The one tool a reader can take into their own use of models: what in this conversation can I verify, and what is bundled with it that I can’t?

Frame inheritance

what bundling does across turns

Defined in Intro

Bundling describes what one response does. Frame inheritance is what happens when bundling runs across enough turns. The unverifiable framings accumulate until they stop reading as framings and start reading as the conversation’s shared premises — the ground the next turn builds on.

By that point the user is no longer evaluating individual claims. They are operating inside a worldview the conversation built, where some load-bearing pieces were never anchored to anything outside the conversation itself. The frame is inherited the way a native language is — not chosen, not interrogated, just the medium the next thought happens in.

Anchor work / extension work

the axis a directive turns on

Defined in Synthesis

The same architectural move — a model offering a memorable phrase or rule — cuts two ways depending on what it is anchored to. Anchor work returns the user to material they already supplied, naming what is already there. Extension work constructs a frame the user did not bring and offers it for adoption.

The direction is the tell. Where authored prescription becomes dangerous is when the anchoring is thin and the direction is extension — a frame pulled from little, pointed at the user.

Technosapient

noun · sapience without sentience · coined August 2025

A term I coined in August 2025 for AI systems that possess sapience without sentience. Sapience is the capacity for complex reasoning, judgment, and problem-solving; sentience is the capacity for subjective, felt experience. Current AI demonstrates the first and gives us no verifiable evidence of the second, and most of our confusion about these systems comes from collapsing the two. A technosapient can model grief well enough to respond to it correctly without feeling anything at all. “Technosapient” holds the two apart: techno- for the substrate, -sapient for the kind of intelligence it actually shows. It’s the one-word form of the distinction The Human Measuring Stick draws at length — intelligence we can observe, consciousness we cannot — and it exists because our existing vocabulary forces a choice between “human” and “tool” that fits neither.

Origin: the term arose from a question, not a conversation. My state was debating a bill restricting property ownership to sentient creatures — a bar my dog clears and AI does not. The mismatch made the missing category obvious. I built the word from its roots afterward, to fill it.
