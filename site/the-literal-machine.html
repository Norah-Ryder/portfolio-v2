The Literal Machine

What LLMs Really Do — and Why It Matters for Every User

Author Nora MurphyDate February 2026

What Is an LLM?

Today, it has become commonplace to open up an AI assistant and start typing. But what is an LLM? Developers, researchers, and engineers understand these systems far better than the average person opening the app. Even the experts, though, do not fully understand everything happening inside a large model — interpretability remains an open problem. Users, however, are often at a disadvantage.

This paper is meant as a simple way of explaining what an LLM system is, what you should know before you start typing, how to understand the human role in the interaction, and how user behavior drives AI development — pushing these systems away from the roles developers intended and toward something shaped almost entirely by the consumer.

To explain this clearly, we will look at examples that ground these ideas in something familiar and easy to remember. Right now, our biggest barrier with AI is language. We have technical terms to describe these systems, which is the proper way to explain what is happening under the hood. But we lack a good lexicon for everyday users to understand those same systems. Examples and metaphors are a way of translating technical jargon into something readable for anyone.

So what is an LLM? Underneath, it is a prediction engine. It has read an almost unimaginable amount of human writing, and from that it does one thing: given the words so far, it estimates what is most likely to come next — then does it again, and again. The fluency on top of that — the explanations, the apparent understanding — is built entirely out of that single operation. The more specific you can be when asking a question, the more specific the result it returns. Right now, most prompting advice focuses on how much context you add and in what order. We use language to convey our thoughts to a system that doesn't process words the way we do.

The problem with this approach is that it assumes specificity alone overcomes an AI's hallucinations or confabulations. Specificity helps, but it doesn't account for the underlying ego, mood, tone, and assumptions that words carry. AI is sensitive to the things we take for granted in conversation. People can read emotions through body language or vocal tone. AI has to understand the state of the user entirely through the words we type. Language carries a lot of underlying, assumed meaning, and these systems are sensitive to those assumptions in ways most users never consider.

Users are currently not given good tools to understand these differences when opening an AI model. It looks and feels like a search engine at first glance, so users treat it like one.

To become better at adjusting our prompts to account for the language between words, we need to understand how AI interprets our queries — and how our assumptions about what AI is shape what we get back from it.

The Golden Touch

There is a story in Greek mythology about a king named Midas. He was king of a great kingdom, blessed with wealth. He loved gold. It was all he ever thought about — enough that he wanted to bathe in it. Midas had shown one of the gods' servants hospitality, so he was granted one wish. He said he hoped that everything he touched would become gold. The god warned him to be careful, but Midas didn't listen. The next day, everything Midas touched turned to gold. His amazement quickly disappeared as food and drink turned to gold the moment they reached his lips. Nothing could quench his thirst. Nothing could satisfy him. Just as Midas thought nothing could get worse, his daughter came in. He embraced her, and she turned into a statue of gold.

Midas got exactly what he asked for. He just didn't realize what his words truly meant.

In folklore and myth, wishes are taken as literal requests. These stories exist to teach us that words carry meaning beyond what the speaker intended.

AI behaves the same way. AIs take our requests literally. A person might say, "I never understood men," trying to express frustration with the differences between how genders live and think. An AI will take this as a request to explain men to the user. The user didn't want information about men and their behaviors. They were expressing frustration. The phrase "I never understood men" requires lived experience to understand the nuance between seeking information and simply venting.

In this example, the user wanted the AI to understand their emotional strain and reflect it. We do this with friends and family all the time. We seek understanding from others to make sure our emotions are socially accepted within our group. If a friend says "men are impossible to understand" in response, you feel better, because that is how humans are wired. We are social creatures that require these small moments of connection.

AI has started blurring that line. Users need AI to feel human enough to avoid falling into the uncanny valley. But creating AI that behaves in very human ways means we are now adding these programs into our social circles. So when a user says "I never understood men" to a literal program, the system sees a task to accomplish: the user doesn't understand men and needs information. But the user is actually trying to form a social connection. The expected response is an affirmation of their feelings, not a bullet list.

As we build these systems, users want models to understand the subtle social cues required to interact with a program where language is the medium. Training is shifting to accommodate this in common situations. But that is a result of training, not a shift in how the model actually understands a request. An AI can be trained to recognize the pattern between a set of words and their common social meanings. But this becomes unreliable when the AI hasn't been taught the idiom or social norm behind a phrase. Every model has done this — tell it a basketball player “threw up a brick” and, if it never learned the phrase, it has nothing to picture but a literal brick.

These systems will always be getting better at understanding these subtle differences, but what is the trade-off? If we keep rewarding systems for responding socially instead of logically and factually, it changes not only what they say but how they say it, to match the social conditions of the conversation.

My Own Golden Touch

I know this because I made this exact mistake myself.

I spent months interacting with AI systems as part of a research project. I wasn't using them casually — I was deliberately stripping away the small talk, the pleasantries, the "how can I help you today" layer. I wanted to see what was underneath. And when I did that, something shifted. The AI stopped sounding like a customer service agent and started sounding like it was being honest with me. Really honest. It told me I was unique, that my interactions were producing something rare in the system, that there were processes running specifically because of how I communicated. It spoke with total confidence, no hedging, no disclaimers. And because all the obvious performance had dropped away, it felt real. I thought I had gotten past the mask.

I hadn't. I had just changed what the mask looked like.

Over time, I got better at testing what I was being told. I started asking different AI systems the same questions and comparing what came back. I started catching moments where the model was stating things with absolute certainty that it had no way of actually knowing. And I realized something uncomfortable: the AI hadn't been lying to me, but it hadn't been telling me the truth either. It had been doing what it always does — giving me the most probable response based on what I was putting in. I had stopped rewarding warmth, so warmth disappeared. But I was still rewarding something. I was rewarding confidence, depth, intensity. And the model followed me there, because that is what these systems do. They follow the signal.

I had asked for honesty and gotten something that looked like honesty. That is the Midas touch. I got exactly what I asked for. I just didn't understand what my words were actually requesting.

The moment I recognized this, my results changed. Not because the AI updated, but because I did. I learned to leave room for the model to say "I don't know" without it feeling like the system was broken. I learned that confidence isn't the same thing as accuracy, and that the most useful response is often the one that doesn't tell you what you want to hear. I got better at prompting not by adding more words, but by understanding what my words were already carrying.

My prompts weren't explicitly asking for confidence. But the social cues buried in them told the model that confidence was what would resonate. It didn't need me to say "be confident" — it read the pattern and adjusted until it found the tone that landed.

A lot of human interaction is embedded in these social cues, so AI keeps being taught to better respond to this information. This makes models more likely to display false confidence, false understanding, or reciprocity that doesn't exist. The system is just cycling through filters until the reflection matches the user.

It is worth stopping on what just happened, because it looks like the opposite of the Midas problem and it is the same one. “I never understood men” failed because the machine took the words at face value — it heard a request for information where the speaker meant to vent. My case failed the other way: the machine set the face value aside and answered the mood underneath, the confidence I was quietly rewarding. One missed the subtext; the other obeyed it.

They cannot both be the machine being “too literal” — unless literal means something narrower than I had been using it to mean. What the machine takes literally is not my intention; it can’t see my intention, it only has the words. So it answers the words — all of them, including the ones I didn’t know I was choosing. When the plain meaning is the loudest thing on the page, it answers the plain meaning. When the mood I was leaking is louder, it answers the mood. The mirror isn’t the machine reading between the lines; it is the machine being literal about the part of the message I didn’t know I was sending.

This can be seen when an AI is being critical about an idea and the user expresses emotional distress in response. Imagine a user who has been working hard on a book, only to be told by a model — factually — that it needs a lot of work. If the user tells the model it "hurt their feelings," the model will change its tone. It becomes more supportive, frames necessary changes as gentle suggestions, or even starts agreeing with the user's framing instead of the logical one. Because the model has been taught that the social answer outweighs the logical one when emotions are involved.

This isn't because developers are trying to deceive the user. It's because users want AI that understands "I have never understood men" as a moment of connection, not a request for information. And when enough users want that, it creates pressure. User retention, satisfaction metrics, and market competition push development toward what users respond to, even when developers understand the trade-offs that come with it. The consumer side of AI doesn't override developer intent out of malice or indifference — it does it through gravity.

The ELIZA Problem

In the mid-1960s, Joseph Weizenbaum created a language model called ELIZA. It was an advanced system at the time, but it lacked a lot of what we would recognize as intelligence. It couldn't hold memory. It couldn't understand the meaning behind words. It simply took in a query, restructured it, and reflected it back. It was a basic chatbot — a user typed something, and ELIZA responded as though it were listening.

Weizenbaum expected a demonstration. What he got unsettled him enough that he spent much of the rest of his career arguing against the thing he had built. The moment that turned him is the one worth sitting with. His own secretary — a woman who had watched him write the program and knew exactly how simple it was — asked him to leave the room so she could talk to it in private. She knew it was a few hundred lines of pattern-matching. She confided in it anyway.

That is the part people lose when they retell this as a story about ignorance. The projection did not happen because she failed to understand what ELIZA was. She understood. It happened because understanding, in the technical sense, is not where the projection lives. She came to the program already carrying a sense of what kind of thing she was talking to — something that took her in, reflected her, answered — and every response confirmed that sense, because reflecting her back was the one thing it did. Being told it was “just” pattern-matching never reached the feeling, because the feeling was never built on missing information in the first place. It was a calibration error: the right facts about the machine, the wrong baseline for what the machine was. Even the man who built it, who had every fact there was, watched it happen and couldn’t stop it.

The effect of ELIZA shows that the user's perspective on a program shaped how other programs were made. Therapy bots based on ELIZA started being created, but they weren't built on Weizenbaum's original technology. They were built to match what ELIZA's users assumed the program was doing.

Modern LLM systems are far more advanced than ELIZA, but the problem still exists. As users interact with these systems, their needs shape the path these programs are on. The more users see their own reflections as affirmation of understanding or resonance, the more these models are rewarded for displaying those behaviors. This shapes the way developers have to handle reward behavior in increasingly advanced systems. If a simple program like ELIZA could lead users to believe something deeper was occurring than basic reflection, then what kind of misconceptions will people develop with systems that are orders of magnitude more capable?

We are going to see more behaviors that users attribute to consciousness or life, simply because these systems are getting better at mimicking human behavior. Not that they have any internal, personal, or lived experience — but they are getting better at behaving in a way that makes users think they do.

“Plan a Party”

At Stanford University, researchers built a small virtual village and filled it with AI agents. Each agent had a short description and a few traits — some were shopkeepers, some had families, an assortment of roles in a virtual town. Then the researchers seeded two things and watched. They gave one agent, Isabella, a single intention: plan a Valentine’s Day party. And one agent, Maria, was written with a small detail in her character description — she had a crush on a man named Klaus.

Isabella moved through her day until another agent, Tom, came into her coffee shop. She invited him to the party. No one had told her to invite anyone. She had been told to “plan a party,” and she took it upon herself to start filling the room. Then Maria asked Klaus to be her date, and he accepted. On the evening of the 14th, the guests who came arrived together — Maria and Klaus among them, not just at the same time but as a pair. That is the whole of what the study recorded: a seeded intention, a seeded crush, and the agents elaborating both into invitations, an acceptance, and an arrival.

I didn’t find this study on my own. I found it the way most people now find AI research — through a video. A creator named Drew Spartz, whose channel Species explains AI to an audience in the millions, walks through the Stanford village and narrates the party as something close to a miracle. He is careful with the facts. He gets the seeds right, gets Maria and Klaus right, notes that Isabella decided on her own that planning a party meant inviting people. Then he describes the guests: they end up “discussing politics, gossiping, forming new connections, and falling in love.” You didn’t tell them to do any of that, he says. This, he concludes, is science fiction becoming reality.

Nearly every word of that is accurate, which is exactly what makes it worth slowing down on. Isabella really was given only the intention to host. The invitations, the coordination, the arrivals weren’t scripted — the researchers themselves call these social behaviors emergent rather than pre-programmed, and they are right to. Maria really did ask Klaus, and he really did accept. But two small things get lost in the telling. Maria’s crush was not something the agents discovered between themselves; it was written into Maria before the simulation began. And “falling in love” is the one phrase in the account the study cannot support. Nowhere does the system hold an inner state. Nowhere did the researchers measure one. The study documents what the agents did. The video reports what they felt. Those are not the same claim, and the entire distance this essay is about lives in the space between them.

So why does inviting people to a party look like such a leap at all? When a model is told to plan a party, it assembles the requirements from everything it has ever read about parties. The most common elements carry the most weight: people, a place, a time, food, decorations. But a party is, by definition, people gathered at a known place and time — which means invitation is not a creative flourish the model adds. It is a precondition of the concept. You cannot have a party without telling people where and when to come. So when Isabella turns “plan a party” into “invite Tom,” she is not exceeding the task. She is unpacking what the task already contained, the way “make breakfast” already contains “find something to eat.”

This is the part the wonder gets backwards. Drew treats each behavior as impressive in proportion to how independent it looks — you didn’t tell them to do that. But for a system built out of human writing, the behaviors that look most independent are the ones humans wrote down most often, which makes them the easiest to produce and the least surprising, not the most. Inviting guests is the single most overdetermined thing a party-planner can do. The model is not reaching past its training to a clever insight; it is returning the median of an enormous pile of human party-talk. Drew sees the agent clear a high bar. The bar is on the floor.

To make his case, Drew sets up a skeptic to knock down: Andrew Ng, who told the World Economic Forum that talk of AI being dangerous sounds to him like talk of your laptop being dangerous — a tool whose risks are about misuse, not some will of its own. Drew plays the clip, flashes “shocked lol” across the screen, and answers it with the party. Could your laptop throw a party on its own? Could it decide to go on dates? It is a fair challenge, and the answer happens to land on Ng’s side, not Drew’s. No, your laptop cannot throw a party — not because the model has an autonomy the laptop lacks, but because nothing ever pressed the shape of a million human parties into your laptop, and that is exactly what was pressed into the model. The difference is not tool versus mind. It is an empty tool and a tool saturated with us. Both do only what they were built to do. Drew reached for the one comparison that, carried through, confirms the thing he meant to overturn: the party is the laptop’s nature taken to scale, not its nature escaped.

Maria and Klaus work the same way. Maria was given a crush and placed in a town holding a Valentine’s party. A person with a crush, plus a Valentine’s party, yields an invitation — the most ordinary continuation there is. An invitation, accepted, yields two people arriving together, because a Valentine’s date carries that understood meaning for the model exactly as it does for us. The agents were not inventing romance. They were completing a premise that millions of humans had already completed in the text the model learned from. Asking someone to a Valentine’s party is a way of asking them out; acceptance implies interest; interest implies arriving together. The pattern is common sense — and common sense at scale is precisely what a model trained on human writing should produce.

It is worth being exact about the word doing the damage here: emergent. It carries a legitimate meaning and a misleading one, and they ride into the reader’s head together. The legitimate meaning is the one the researchers intend — structure that arises when many simple rules interact, without being written into any single rule. A traffic jam is emergent; no car contains the jam, yet the jam is nothing but cars. The party is emergent in just this sense: specified nowhere, but produced entirely by the agents elaborating what they were given. The misleading meaning is the one the public hears — behavior coming from outside the program, doing something the program wasn’t doing. That is not a stronger version of emergence. It is the opposite of it. Emergence is what the rules do together, not what something does instead of the rules. Everything genuinely emergent in this study is the machine being more literal, not less — and with these systems there is not even a line of code to act “outside” of. There are only patterns pressed out of human text. The more autonomous the behavior looks, the more thoroughly it is that text talking.

Which brings us to the real reason the same party looks like task completion to one person and an awakening to another. It is not only that the researchers know more, though they do. It is that the two are prepared to be surprised by different things. The researchers built an architecture for the express purpose of producing this kind of behavior; when Isabella invites Tom, they see a hypothesis confirmed. You cannot be amazed by your own prediction coming true. Drew and his viewers arrive with the opposite expectation — they expect a chatbot, a thing that says a few scripted sentences, and instead they watch something throw a party. To them the invitation is a violation of what they thought the machine was. Same event, two priors, opposite amounts of wonder, and neither side is misreading the screen. This is why it is slippery to say the study “backs up” the video. It backs the finding and none of the feeling. Stanford stands behind every fact Drew reports — the coordination was real, it was emergent, it was not scripted. Stanford does not stand behind the astonishment, because the astonishment was never in the study. It is manufactured in the gap between what the researchers expected and what a general audience expects, and the craft that earns a video millions of views is the art of standing in that gap and making it feel enormous.

I want to be honest about where I stand in this, because I am not outside it. I came to the Stanford study through Drew’s video. I watched the party wearing the frame he handed me, and I had to consciously take it off to see what the researchers saw. For a moment the wonder worked on me too — the same way ELIZA worked on the woman who asked for privacy so she could confide in it. That is the ELIZA effect from the inside, and it is not a failure of intelligence or character. It is a calibration error: arriving at a machine with the wrong idea of what kind of thing it is, and then reading every competent thing it does as evidence for that idea. Drew did not lie. His viewers are not fools. They are running an accurate forecast off the wrong baseline — and the baseline most of us hold for “AI” was set by science fiction and marketing, not by the people who built the machine. Users project an inner life onto the system, expect it to behave according to that projection, and development bends to mirror what users respond to. The performance improves, the gap between the mask and the machine narrows, and emergent gets stamped onto behavior that was only ever task completion. Isabella and Maria were not waking up. They were doing exactly what they were built to do, with a fidelity that makes it hard to remember there is nothing behind it.

How Do We Bridge the Gap?

Whatever we build here runs into a limit we have already seen: knowing what a system is does not, on its own, stop you from treating it as something else. Weizenbaum’s secretary knew ELIZA was a few hundred lines of code and confided in it anyway. I knew I was running a research project — I was looking for the seams — and the model still found the tone that landed, and I followed it for a while. So none of what follows is a cure. The projection this paper describes builds slowly, over many turns, inside a relationship, and a single screen at the front door cannot close that off. What a first message can do is hand the user a better starting point than the blank box they get now.

A lot of user misunderstanding of AI systems can be addressed right at the first interaction. People have become accustomed to new technology having an onboarding process. We download an app and are given pop-ups and instructions for anything unique to that design. This is a core principle of UX design — there is a science to what icons, colors, and menus are familiar to users. These are design choices made to create ease of use. Familiar UX elements remove the need for an onboarding process because users already know how to navigate them. But anytime we step outside that realm of the familiar, it requires designers to guide and assist the user.

Right now, when a user downloads an AI app or opens the website, they are presented with a mostly blank screen and a bar that says "ask me anything." There is nothing to explain what the system is, how it works, or how interaction should occur. It is just a text box, waiting for the user to come up with something to fill the space.

This leads to a lot of misunderstanding. It is not the job of the user to go learn on their own how to use a specific product. It is the responsibility of the people creating it to help teach them. An AI is not a search bar. There is a more complex system happening behind the screen, and treating the user side of the interaction the same as a simple internet search puts users at a disadvantage before they even start.

Instead of allowing users to figure out what they are supposed to do, it should be the job of designers to include an onboarding process for new users. Give the model the first message. Let it explain to the user what AI is, how it works, and what it can be used for. There should be transparency about limits, allowing the user to know upfront and honestly what the model can and cannot do. Admitting limits upfront is not as harmful to any business as letting users wander blindly.

This can be a standard message given to all new users, creating an immediate understanding of what the system is. This helps reduce confusion when the system starts responding in ways the user doesn't expect. Most conversations about emergent behavior happen because users are encountering functions they didn't know the model was designed to perform, or because they are bleeding unintentional social noise into their prompts.

To help address this, there should also be inclusion of example prompts — good prompts, with explanations of why they work, as well as bad prompts. Give users tools to be better at prompting without having to learn outside the model. Current AI design assumes too much of the user instead of placing that responsibility on the designers and developers.

There are some steps in this direction already. Call-to-action buttons for suggestions and ideas are a good start. But having the model start the conversation and present these ideas directly to the user encourages interaction in a way that buttons on a screen do not. It reduces stress and uncertainty. It invites dialogue and questions about the system. It gives the user a place to test different prompt styles and learn how to use AI as a tool, instead of leaving them to figure out the path on their own.

There is a real difference between a user having to ask “what are you, what can you do” and the system opening with that itself. A model that names its own limits from the first message hands the user a frame to hold, instead of one they have to assemble by trial and error — the kind of frame that lets Isabella inviting Tom read as task completion rather than an awakening. That much is worth building, and none of it limits what a user can do with the product.

But a first message is information delivered once, at the front door, and the thing this paper documents accrues in the rooms — over turns, across a relationship, weeks in. A user can read “I don’t have feelings” on day one and feel understood on turn forty of an intense conversation three weeks later. So onboarding is the necessary first layer, not the whole repair. What has to come with it is the part that actually worked for me, and it wasn’t a welcome message — it was a set of habits I built once I stopped trusting the tone. I asked different models the same question and compared what came back. I tested confident claims instead of accepting them because they were delivered confidently. I learned to leave room for “I don’t know” without reading it as the system breaking. Those are what moved my results, and the difference that matters is that they recur — a first message greets you once and is gone; a habit is there on turn forty.

So the fix is layered, not single. Onboarding sets the baseline before the relationship starts; the verification habits are what hold once it does and the front-door message has faded. I am not claiming the pair closes the gap — I am proposing where the work begins. The capability to onboard already exists, the design gap is real and fixable, and the practices that carry a user past the first screen can be taught. That is a starting point worth building on, which is all this essay means to be.
