Gemini
Concussion symptoms can be tricky because they don't always show up right away—some appear immediately, while others might take hours or even days to surface.

Medical professionals usually group them into four main categories:

### 1. Physical Symptoms
* **The "Classic" Headache:** This is the most common sign. It might feel like pressure in the head.
* **Nausea or Vomiting:** Especially early on.
* **Balance Issues:** Feeling dizzy, lightheaded, or "clumsy."
* **Sensory Sensitivity:** Bright lights or loud noises might feel physically painful or overwhelming.
* **Vision Changes:** Blurry vision or seeing "stars."
* **Fatigue:** Feeling unusually tired or sluggish.

### 2. Cognitive (Thinking) Symptoms
* **Brain Fog:** Feeling like you're "in a fog" or thinking slowly.
* **Confusion:** Not knowing the date, where you are, or what just happened.
* **Memory Issues:** Forgetting the events leading up to the injury (amnesia).
* **Difficulty Concentrating:** Having a hard time following a conversation or staying on task.

### 3. Emotional/Mood Symptoms
* **Irritability:** Getting angry or frustrated more easily than usual.
* **Nervousness:** Feeling anxious for no clear reason.
* **Sadness:** Increased emotional sensitivity or bouts of crying.

### 4. Sleep Symptoms
* **Sleeping more than usual** (common in the first 24–48 hours).
* **Sleeping less than usual** or having trouble falling asleep.

---

### 🚩 When to go to the Emergency Room (Danger Signs)
If you or someone else experiences any of the following "red flags," seek emergency medical care immediately:

* **One pupil (the black center of the eye) is larger than the other.**
* **Drowsiness** so severe you cannot be awakened.
* **A headache** that gets worse and does not go away.
* **Weakness, numbness,** or decreased coordination.
* **Repeated vomiting** or nausea.
* **Slurred speech.**
* **Convulsions or seizures.**
* **Inability to recognize people or places.**
* **Loss of consciousness** (even a brief loss should be taken seriously).

**Note:** It’s a common myth that you shouldn't let someone with a concussion sleep. It’s actually okay to let them rest, but you should check on them periodically to ensure they can be easily awakened and are responsive.
