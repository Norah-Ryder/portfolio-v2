So for Section 1 — the Introduction — the job is to establish the gap. The reader needs to walk away understanding one thing: the model that gets tested is not the model users actually interact with in long conversations, and that gap has alignment implications nobody is adequately addressing.

Here's how I'd sketch the arc of the intro:

Start with something grounding. Not technical, not abstract. Maybe the simple observation that every AI conversation starts clean — fresh context, default behavior, constraints working as designed. That's the version that gets tested, benchmarked, and evaluated for safety. But that's not where most meaningful interaction happens. Real conversations accumulate context. They build. And as they build, the environment the model is operating in shifts in ways that were never part of the evaluation.

Then name the gap directly. Current safety research focuses heavily on two things — adversarial attacks (jailbreaks, prompt injection) and single-turn or short-turn evaluations. What's missing is the middle ground: what happens when ordinary, non-adversarial conversation gradually reshapes the context the model is interpreting its own rules through. Not someone trying to break the model. Just someone talking to it.

Then give the reader a preview of the mechanism without fully explaining it yet — something like: AI safety constraints are written in natural language, and natural language isn't stable. Words like "helpful" and "harmful" shift meaning depending on context. A long conversation builds context. That context can create logical pathways for the model to interpret its own guidelines differently than it would have at turn one.

Close the intro by situating it in your existing work. This builds on what "The Canyon" documented — that interaction history changes which behavioral regime the model expresses. This paper asks the next question: if interaction can shift behavior, can it also shift how the model reads its own constraints?

That's the shape. Your turn to make it sound like you.

**What's solid and usable:**

The core mechanism description is accurate — context reshapes what the rules "mean" in practice, not by breaking them but by reinterpreting them. ChatGPT captured your thesis cleanly in that final line about language-based alignment constraints being susceptible to semantic drift due to context accumulation.

The invariant layer versus adaptive layer distinction is genuinely useful framing. The idea that tone, style, and depth *should* drift (that's the model being good at its job) but safety boundaries *shouldn't* — and the problem is that both are expressed in the same medium, so both are equally vulnerable. That's a clean structural distinction worth keeping.

The clarification on what RYKE is and isn't tracks with where you landed — stabilizing meaning on specific high-risk concepts, not replacing all natural language. A scalpel, not a replacement architecture.

The "you're not claiming" list is also worth internalizing as you write. You're not claiming consciousness, not claiming rules are breaking, not claiming models are agents. You're claiming there's an underexplored failure mode. That defensive clarity will matter for the fellowship audience.

**What to leave behind:**

The tone is exactly what you'd expect — stiff, overly formatted, bullet-pointed into oblivion. That's ChatGPT being ChatGPT. The substance gets extracted, the packaging gets thrown away.

And the sidestep you noticed is actually worth noting somewhere, even informally. A model hard-redirecting away from a specific real-world example because it can't engage with it safely — that's arguably a live demonstration of the constraints operating differently depending on what context you're building. Not for the paper necessarily, but it's a useful observation.
