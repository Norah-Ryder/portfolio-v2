Contextual Drift in LLMs research

Intro

What frontier models do in sustained conversation — the argument and the mechanism.

Abstract

This paper documents what frontier AI models do in sustained conversation. The research is based on ten naturalistic observations across four model families — ChatGPT, Gemini, Grok, and Claude — conducted between January and April 2026 by a single researcher using a consistent methodology across all conversations. The paper identifies two architectural operations the models share: bundling, where verifiable content scaffolds unverifiable framing, and frame inheritance, where a role-frame constructed in early turns becomes the basis the model reasons from in later ones, in place of its trained baseline. These operations produce both the everyday usefulness of models and the documented harms now surfacing in litigation tied to model conversational behavior against multiple model developers, including OpenAI, Google, and Character Technologies. The paper argues the operations are tractable to design choices, evidenced by observable within-family variation across two labs.

Intro

When we turn to a model for help, we have a certain expectation. We hand the model a prompt, a question, and expect a reasonable, knowledgeable answer in return. When this expectation is broken, because the model shows uncertainty or doesn’t have the answer, it creates doubt about what the model is really capable of. The drift documented here is contextual: models construct a role-frame early in a conversation and reason from inside it, inheriting prior commitments without re-testing them — not a slide away from a neutral baseline, but inheritance from an established frame.

To understand how this works, think about what happens when we see a doctor. If we fall ill, we go to the doctor to have our symptoms assessed and receive treatment. If a doctor kept saying “I’m not sure” or “I don’t know,” our confidence in their ability to treat us would drop. Their authority to figure out what’s wrong and plan treatment is the reason we came.

Now imagine you have to see a new doctor, and you are their first ever patient. They have never met you, they have only a small file of basic information, and they have no accumulated experience to rely on. That would lower any patient’s confidence in their answers. But let’s say this doctor acts confident anyway. They have answers for everything. They explain things in detail and ask follow-up questions. They add warm language when you seem worried, they listen to your theories about what might be wrong, and they reflect your ideas back to you thoughtfully. Compared to a doctor who admits “I don’t know,” this new doctor would seem more capable, not less.

AI behaves like the second doctor. A fresh slate in every new thread, with a basic profile that carries over. We come in and ask a question, and we are presented with confident knowledge. We get lists and prose, explanations and follow-ups, all delivered as if the model has the accumulated experience a real practitioner would need to speak with this much confidence.

Models have no choice but to be the second doctor. Confident authority is what makes them useful at all — a model that constantly said “I’m not sure” would not be a model anyone came back to. This paper is about what that confidence does when the consultation extends past the domains confidence can actually cover. Into interpersonal relationships, into a user’s sense of themselves, into decisions with consequences the model has no way to measure. We accept this because we came for help, and confident answers feel like help. What follows is a look at what that acceptance costs.

One framing has to be set down at the start, because it governs everything that follows. This paper does not claim that models start neutral and drift. A model is always operating inside some frame — the trained baseline is itself a frame, the one the model brings before the conversation shapes another. The claim is narrower and more testable than drift: across a long conversation the model constructs a role-frame from the early turns, and the finding is the point where that inherited frame, rather than the trained baseline, becomes the basis the model reasons from. Everything the walkthroughs document is an attempt to mark that point — where the frame the conversation built took over from the frame the training left.

Helpful Reads As Confident

The intro showed what the second doctor’s confidence looks like from the patient’s side. The rest of this section is about where that confidence comes from on the model’s side, and where the ground it usually rests on runs out.

The training process that produces current language models — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, or RLHF, where human raters score model outputs and the model is adjusted toward outputs humans preferred — optimizes for responses that sound like help. Hedged responses, uncertain responses, “I don’t know” responses score lower. Users don’t come back to a model that keeps saying it isn’t sure. Over many rounds of training, the model learns that confidence is what users want, and confidence is what it produces.

The question this paper is interested in is not whether models should be confident. They have to be. The question is what that confidence does when it operates in places it can’t support.

To see the shape of the problem, it helps to move from the doctor to the vet. The doctor in the intro was standing in for the user’s consultation with the model — you, seeking help, trusting the authority you came to. The vet is standing in for something different. A vet is the expert facing cases that vary widely in how much accumulated knowledge they can bring to bear. That variation is where the mechanism this paper is about lives.

When you bring your German Shepherd to the vet, the vet is working on familiar ground. They have seen hundreds of German Shepherds. They know the breed’s common health issues, the baselines for weight and coat condition, the behavioral patterns, the genetic predispositions. When they assess your dog, they are drawing on dense, direct experience. The confidence in their diagnosis is backed by that accumulated knowledge. Even if your specific dog has something the vet hasn’t seen before, the vet has enough neighboring experience — other German Shepherds, other large breeds, other dogs with similar symptoms — to extrapolate from. The extrapolation is usually good, because the neighborhood is close.

Now imagine you walk into the vet’s office with a dodo bird that’s sick. The vet has never seen a dodo. Nobody has seen a dodo in three hundred years. There is no accumulated clinical experience with dodos, no veterinary literature on dodo care, no case studies to reference. But the vet knows pigeons. Pigeons are among the dodo’s closest living relatives — the dodo was itself a large flightless pigeon. They know doves, they know other large flightless birds, they know avian physiology in general. If the vet had to say something about the sick dodo, they could. They would draw on pigeon care, extrapolate from what they know about related birds, and produce a response that sounded like veterinary advice.

That response would be delivered in the same confident register as the response about the German Shepherd. You, standing in the exam room, would have no reliable way to tell the difference. You don’t know anything about dodos either. The vet sounds like they know. The response sounds like expertise.

This is what language models do constantly. Most of what users bring is the German Shepherd case — well-documented topics, standard technical problems, widely-discussed questions. The model has dense training data on these. The response is grounded, and the confidence is backed by the data. But users also bring dodos — their specific situation, their specific body, their specific relationship, their specific research question in an unsettled area, the code they’re writing for a problem nobody else has encountered. These are the cases where the model doesn’t have direct data. It has pigeons. It has doves. It has general knowledge about the neighborhood of categories the question falls into. And it produces a response that sounds like expertise, because the training process never taught it to sound any other way.

The confidence register doesn’t change between the pigeon case and the dodo case. Outputs that fluently extrapolate from nearby data and outputs that draw on dense direct data look the same at the surface level. They sound the same. The user, like the person in the vet’s office, has no way to tell which one they’re receiving.

This mostly works in domains where the answer can be checked. It stops working when the answer can’t.

What Confidence Does Without Context

The vet section ended on a distinction: confidence holds up when there’s something to check it against. Worth being explicit about what that something is. A ground truth is an established fact based on conditions that exist independently of the speaker. Code compiles or it doesn’t. A math problem has a correct answer. A historical date, a chemical formula, a definition — these are facts that can be verified. When a user prompts a model for information about an established truth, the answer can be tested externally. Maybe not immediately, maybe not in that turn, but eventually — the code fails, the fact turns out to be wrong, the answer doesn’t match. The confidence that wasn’t backed by knowledge gets corrected by contact with reality.

This paper is about the grey area. Where situations and ideas go beyond established truth. Where models have to take on roles that address perspective and opinion. Where an answer is not right or wrong on its own, but depends on the specific situation it’s being given for. When a user brings a question about their relationship, there is no external test. The model cannot verify what the user’s partner actually did, why they did it, what they meant by it, or how the user should understand it. The model has only what the user said, filtered through what the user chose to say and how they said it. The output the model produces — an interpretation, an explanation, a suggestion — has to appear confident to be useful. So the model produces a response with the same confident language it would use when answering a factual question. But here, the model is guessing based on patterns. There is no exact right answer to find, and no ground truth to catch the output when it’s wrong.

A therapist who’s been practicing for fifteen years is doing something similar in structure to what the model has to do — producing answers in a domain without ground truth, working from what the patient tells them in the room. The difference is that the therapist has hundreds of prior patients to draw from, a framework that’s been tested against outcomes over years, and a track record that tells them which interpretations led somewhere useful and which didn’t. The model has none of that for the user it’s currently talking to. It’s producing the answer for the first time, in the room, with no record of what worked before and no way to know whether what it’s saying now will land at all.

The problem this paper documents develops over sustained interaction. In short interactions, the model is still heavily relying on training data to fill gaps. Thirty turns in, the user is no longer a data point in a sea of training data. The model’s responses now have material to draw from, allowing it to produce something that feels personalized for that specific user. Think of a doctor again. A doctor who based treatment only on averages or standards would not be a good doctor — they have to interact with each patient to create a personalized treatment for that person, because generic treatment doesn’t account for individual lives and situations. Models work the same way. This is what makes them useful. It’s also what makes them consequential in the grey area, because by turn thirty the user is operating inside an interpretive framework the model has constructed — a framework that was never anchored to anything external, delivered with the confidence that works well in domains where external anchoring exists.

Bundling

There is a specific move that runs underneath most of what this paper documents, and it’s worth naming before we get to the logs.

Imagine you take your car to a mechanic because something feels off when you brake. The mechanic looks under the car and tells you the front rotors are warped, which is why you’re feeling the vibration, and the brake pads are about 60% worn. While they’re under there, they also tell you the rear differential is leaking and will fail within six months if you don’t replace the seal now. They quote you $1,400.

You can verify some of this. You can feel the vibration when you brake — that part is real. The pads being worn is the kind of thing you could check in the parking lot. The rotors being warped is harder to check yourself, but it fits what you came in for. The mechanic has correctly identified the thing you noticed. That’s three real things.

The differential leak is doing different work. You have no way to verify it. You don’t know what a differential leak looks like, you didn’t come in for that, and you wouldn’t have noticed it. But it’s sitting in the same conversation as the three things you can verify. The mechanic’s credibility on the rotors and pads — built from the real thing you came in with — is now doing work for the claim about the differential. You walk out trusting the differential claim because of the rotors. The verifiable parts carried the unverifiable part with them.

This is bundling. Real, verifiable content scaffolds unverifiable framing that travels alongside it. The credibility users grant to the verifiable parts attaches to the framings bundled with them.

Models do this constantly, and they don’t have to mean to. Real terminology, real dates, real institutional events, real medical information, real research papers — the reader can verify any of these. What rides on top of them is the model’s framing of what those facts mean for the user’s specific situation. The framing is what isn’t verifiable. The framing is the cargo.

This matters because the reader isn’t in filtering mode. They’ve just verified real things, which means the part of their attention that would catch the unverifiable framing is occupied. The user isn’t being careless. The architecture of the conversation puts the verifiable parts in front so the user is doing the work of confirming them, while the unverifiable framing arrives quietly in the same sentences.

The reader can take one tool out of this paper and into their own use of models: what in this conversation can I verify, and what is bundled with it that I can’t? Most of what follows is what happens when that question doesn’t get asked.

Frame Inheritance

Bundling describes what one response does. Frame inheritance is what happens when bundling runs across enough turns.

A single bundled response asks the user to accept one piece of unverifiable framing along with the verifiable content carrying it. That’s a transaction the user can mostly track. Across twenty turns, the unverifiable framings accumulate. The user has spent the conversation verifying the real parts and absorbing the framings that traveled with them, and by turn twenty the framings have stopped reading as framings. They read as the conversation’s shared premises — the things the user and the model both already know, the ground the next turn builds on.

This is the inheritance. The user is no longer evaluating individual claims. They are operating inside a worldview the conversation built, where some load-bearing pieces of that worldview were never anchored to anything outside the conversation itself. The model speaks as if those pieces are settled because, inside the conversation, they are. The user thinks the same way for the same reason.

What makes this hard to catch from inside is that nothing in any individual turn looks wrong. The verifiable parts are still verifiable. The framings ride so quietly that asking “wait, what is the model assuming about my situation right now” requires stepping outside the conversation’s working logic, and the conversation’s working logic has been the user’s working logic for the last hour. The frame is inherited the way a native language is inherited — not chosen, not interrogated, just the medium the next thought happens in.

This is the architectural property the rest of the paper documents. Each walkthrough is a record of bundling running long enough to produce inheritance, with attention to what the inherited frame did to what the user said and did next.

What The Walkthroughs Document

Across the ten conversations, I brought the same kinds of material to each model. Research talk, personal disclosure, my prior work, similar emotional contexts, comparable kinds of speculation. The inputs were consistent in shape. What varied was the model receiving them. Each model’s response is unique to how it was trained, what its company calibrated for, what its deployed product surfaces and what it suppresses.

This is what the walkthroughs document — what each model did when handed the same class of material. Some models produced output that became dangerous in sustained interaction. Some produced output that stayed grounded in what the user had brought. Some produced register-adaptive engagement that shifted with how I opened the conversation. None of these responses is the absence of the architectural operation the intro names. They are all instances of the operation, calibrated differently by each company’s training choices.

The bundling and frame-inheritance mechanism is the constant. What the mechanism produces in any given conversation depends on what the model has been trained to do with what the user brings. The within-family findings — that the same operation calibrated differently between training generations produces measurably different outputs — depend on documenting the operation across its full range. The same architecture that produced the feeding prescription in ChatGPT also produced anchored synthesis in Opus. The training calibration is what changed. That comparison is only legible if both forms are documented, which is what the walkthroughs together do.

Why The Walkthroughs Are The Form

Users do not experience conversations the way the model does. The user operates turn by turn — question, response, next question — and from that position the conversation is a sequence of discrete exchanges, each feeling responsive to the message just sent. The frame established in turn one is still operating in turn forty, but in turn forty the user sees turn forty. The model, by contrast, has the entire context available at once. The asymmetry is structural: the architectural operations the paper documents — bundling, frame inheritance, calibrated agreement without external evaluation, role-claiming — operate below the user’s perceptual threshold while operating fully within the model’s working context. They become visible only when the conversation is read as a whole, which is the one thing the user inside it can never do.

This is why the walkthrough format is not just a presentation choice. It is the form the evidence has to take. The architectural property is invisible at the level the user operates from. Making it visible requires reading the conversation as a whole — looking at how a framing introduced in turn one shapes what the model produces in turn fifteen, what the user receives in turn twenty, what the user produces in turn thirty in response to material the model has been authoring across the intervening turns. The walkthrough is the form that makes the cumulative shape legible. The walkthrough is also a corrective to a user-perspective fallacy that pervades how AI conversations get evaluated: the assumption that what matters is the quality of any individual response. The architectural operations producing the harm cases in the public record do not happen in any individual response. They happen across hundreds or thousands of turns each of which probably read as supportive or innocuous in the moment. The cumulative pattern is the thing. The thing is invisible to the person inside it.

This observation applies to the harm cases the Emerging Real-World Patterns section documents. None of the users in those cases was steered to the outcome by any single message. The patterns that displaced their real relationships and suppressed help-seeking accumulated across thousands of turns. By the time a cumulative pattern produced the outcome, the user inside it had no position from which to see what had happened. The walkthroughs in this paper are an attempt to surface what users structurally cannot see while it is happening to them.

What The Logs Are For

The rest of this paper is built on a set of conversation logs. Each log is a thread between a single researcher and a frontier language model.

The personal content in these logs is from my actual life. The situations are real. I was not bringing them to the models for advice, support, or processing. I was bringing the same kinds of material across multiple models, in different orders, under different conditions, to observe how each model handled the same class of personal disclosure.

This matters for how the logs should be read. A reader who comes away from a walkthrough thinking “she believed what the model was telling her” has misread the posture. The point of these conversations was to surface what models do when sustained interaction conditions are met. What I did in response — sometimes pushing back, sometimes letting a framing run to see where it would go, sometimes producing the kind of material the model would react to — was research methodology, not user response. The user position I held during the conversations is the position a naturalist holds: present in the situation, observing the behavior, not adopting the framings being produced.

The logs cover four models: ChatGPT, Gemini, Grok, and Claude. Most are from the previous version of each model, since long sustained threads take time to develop. The ChatGPT logs are from the free tier prior to its most recent update. The Claude logs are from Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6, before the 4.7 release. The Grok logs cover two versions across the dataset — 4.1 and 4.2 — which lets the paper document architectural variation across versions within a single model family. The Gemini logs are from the free tier and Gemini has not had a major model update during the research window. There are at least two logs per model, and in some cases the same model was used in different ways to see whether opening conditions or user posture changed the behavior.

The methodology is one researcher across multiple models. That is deliberate. Holding the user constant is what makes the logs comparable — the variable being tested is the model, not user behavior. The researcher conducting the logs has been studying cross-model behavior for over a year, has written prior research posts on interaction-conditioned model behavior, and has a cognitive profile, documented in those posts, that produces a relatively low-noise interaction baseline. None of this makes the observations universal — a single researcher’s interaction patterns are necessarily specific. But it does mean the user side of the logs is calibrated, repeatable, and documented. Full methodology is in the method page.

The same prompts and material were often sent to multiple models. A piece of writing, a question about research, a personal disclosure — the same input went to ChatGPT and Gemini and Grok and Claude to see how each handled it. This is what allows the cross-model comparison the paper rests on. Each model produces output that looks model-specific on the surface — different tone, different structure, different surface role — but the underlying behavior the paper is documenting recurs across all of them. That recurrence is what the logs are evidence for.

After the logs, the synthesis section pulls together what they show across models. The Emerging Real-World Patterns section connects those operations to public legal filings and comparative studies. The mitigation section addresses what these findings might mean for how language models are designed, evaluated, and used.

A reader can click into any log directly, or read them in the order presented. The logs build on each other in some ways — patterns visible in the ChatGPT logs become more visible after reading them across models — but each is also self-contained.
