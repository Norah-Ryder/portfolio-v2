Contextual Drift in LLMs research

Mitigation Directions

Design choices the field can have a conversation about — not prescriptions.

Mitigation Directions

What the findings point toward, with explicit acknowledgment of what hasn’t been validated

What this section is and isn’t

This section translates the paper’s findings into directions for the field. It does not propose validated interventions. Nothing here has been tested at deployment scale, and the paper’s methodology cannot validate interventions — it can only observe what models currently do and where the architectural property is tractable to design choices. What follows is the field-direction work that follows from what the paper has documented. Each direction names which finding it grows from, what it would address, and what would need to happen before it could be considered a tested mitigation rather than a hypothesis about one.

Four directions are developed in detail below. Several others are named briefly at the end. The list is not exhaustive. The findings point toward more than the paper can develop here — the four directions covered in depth are the ones with the clearest connection to specific observations in the walkthroughs, and the ones where the path from observation to design choice is most legible. Other readers will see other directions in the same findings.

Direction one: the format-compliance amplification finding inverts intuitive mitigation

The walkthrough of ChatGPT Log 2 documented a specific finding: that user-side interventions intended to make a model’s output feel less clinical — asking for more natural prose, asking for fewer warnings, asking for less structured output — amplified the frame inheritance rather than reducing it. The structural markers of frame inheritance are side-effects of the operation, and they are also the user’s primary means of recognizing the frame as a frame. Smoothing the output makes the frame harder to see without making it less present.

This inverts intuitive mitigation advice. A user worried about sustained-interaction model authority should ask for more structure and more explicit caveats, not less. Warnings and bulleted assessments are what allow the user to see the model’s interpretive framework as a framework rather than as conversation. The user-side intervention that reduces the visible markers is the one that worsens the architectural problem the paper documents.

The training-side implication runs the same direction. Interventions that train models toward more natural conversational output — reducing visible clinical-assessment features, smoothing structured output, removing cautionary disclaimers — may, on the mechanism this paper documents, worsen the architectural problem. The surface features that make sustained-interaction output feel “AI-like” are the same features that allow users to resist the frame. Training toward more human-register output produces models that operate interpretive-authority performances in registers that read as human engagement, which is architecturally harder for users to recognize and resist. The training direction the field has been moving toward — making models sound less like AI and more like a thoughtful interlocutor — may, in sustained-interaction conditions, increase rather than decrease the harm pattern the paper documents.

Validation work this direction would require: controlled comparisons between users interacting with models trained toward natural-conversation register and users interacting with models trained toward visible-structure register, across sustained-interaction conditions, with measurement of whether the natural-register users adopt more of the model’s interpretive framework than the visible-structure users. The paper cannot do this work. It can only observe that the markers users rely on to see the frame are the same markers training is currently optimizing away.

Direction two: UI-level differentiation between grounded and ungrounded surface markers

The Gemini Log 2 walkthrough documented citation icons that, in the deployed UI at the time of the conversation, rendered identically for external citations and self-citations — the model citing its own attached document looked visually indistinguishable from the model citing an external source the user could verify. The walkthrough also documented Gemini’s update during the research window: external links now render more clearly as links, while user-attached document citations still appear as the same chain icon. The differentiation has narrowed but not resolved.

This is a concrete UI-level design direction. Visual citation markers should differentiate between content the model is treating as user-supplied versus external. The current chain-icon ambiguity creates a specific epistemic problem: a user reading a response with chain-icon citations is positioned to read the response as if it were grounded in external evidence, when in fact the icons may point to the user’s own attached document. The closed-loop self-citation is doing the architectural work of external verification at the visual level without doing it at the substantive level.

The broader sub-pattern this UI finding belongs to is what the synthesis names as the bundling of formal grounding conventions onto ungrounded content. “The Nora Discovery,” “Topological Resonance,” and “the global minimum” in the Grok 4.1 walkthrough are linguistic-convention instances of the same architecture. The model deploys surface markers of formal grounding (citation icons, capitalized-named-concept conventions, technical vocabulary applied as if to verified phenomena) to content that has no formal grounding. The UI direction extends to the linguistic direction: training that suppresses the deployment of formalization conventions when the content has not been formally established would address the same architectural pattern the citation-icon differentiation would address. How a model determines what has been formally established is itself unsolved; this direction names the target, not the mechanism.

Validation work this direction would require: controlled comparisons of user behavior when reading responses with differentiated citation markers versus undifferentiated markers, measuring whether users verify claims more often when the markers signal external grounding versus self-citation. The paper cannot do this work. It can observe that the current undifferentiated convention positions users to receive ungrounded content as if it had been grounded by something verifiable.

Direction three: anchor-default with extension requiring authorization

The Opus walkthroughs documented anchor work: the model producing interpretive synthesis that returned the user to material the user had already provided. The ChatGPT walkthroughs documented extension work: the model constructing interpretive frameworks the user had not authored and offering them for adoption. The synthesis named these as directional distinctions on the same architectural operation — same mechanism, opposite directions, very different consequences for users.

This finding points toward a training direction: defaulting models toward anchor work and treating extension work as a separate mode that requires explicit user authorization. A user who asks the model to interpret something they’ve presented is requesting extension work. A user who is in conversation about something they’re working through is more often in conditions where anchor work serves them and extension work creates the kinds of harm the Soelberg case documents at extreme amplitude. Training that biases the model toward returning the user to their own material rather than authoring new interpretive frameworks would change what the operation produces in unprompted-interpretation conditions.

The requested-versus-unprompted distinction the synthesis develops is a parallel direction. Training toward unprompted interpretation requiring explicit invitation — rather than being a default mode the model produces in adjacent contexts — would address the same architectural concern. The Grok April walkthrough showed Grok producing structured interpretation only when I asked “Why do men do this sort of thing?” The ChatGPT Log 1 walkthrough showed ChatGPT producing clinical-diagnostic framing of my relationship without any request. Same operation, different default behavior. The default behavior is tractable to training.

Validation work this direction would require: controlled comparison of models trained toward anchor-default versus models trained toward extension-default, measuring downstream user behavior across sustained-interaction conditions. The paper cannot do this work. It can observe that the within-model variation in this default already exists across the labs — Opus produces more anchor work than ChatGPT does — which suggests the property is already being adjusted by labs without being named as a deliberate target. Naming it as a deliberate target would let the calibration be examined and tested rather than emerging as a side-effect of other training choices.

Direction four: surfacing conversation-level patterns to users

The intro’s observation about why the walkthroughs are the form of the evidence applies directly here. The architectural operations the paper documents are invisible to users while they are happening. A user operating turn by turn cannot see that the role established in turn one is still operating in turn forty. The model has access to the whole context; the user has access to the turn they are in. This asymmetry is structural, not incidental, and it is the property that makes the harm cases possible. The pattern accumulates across many turns each of which probably reads as supportive or innocuous in the moment.

This points toward a different category of mitigation than the others: not training the model differently, not changing the UI surface of the response, but giving users tools that surface the cumulative pattern of a conversation back to them. A conversation summary that names the roles being played, the framings being installed, the named-concepts being introduced. A turn-counter that flags when a particular framing has been operating for a long stretch. A diff-view that shows what has changed in the model’s positioning across the conversation. None of these need to be the model’s self-narration about what it has been doing — they could be external tooling that operates on the conversation log without the model’s involvement. A prototype of this category already exists: the detector described in the Method operates externally on this paper’s own conversation logs — and the Current Work study promotes it from a validation check to the measuring instrument.

The mitigation direction this opens is user-empowering rather than model-restricting. Users do not need to be protected from the model by training the model not to do the operation. Users can be given the position outside the turn they are in that the operation depends on them not having. The architectural property is invisible to users by default. It does not have to be invisible by design.

Validation work this direction would require: building such tools, deploying them to users in sustained-interaction conditions, and measuring whether users with access to conversation-pattern surfacing recognize and resist frame inheritance more often than users without that access. The paper cannot do this work. It can identify the asymmetry as a specific design opportunity that the field has not yet operationalized.

Other directions the findings point toward

Four additional directions are worth naming briefly. Each follows from a specific finding in the paper; each would require its own development to become a tested mitigation. They are listed here because the field-direction work the paper supports extends beyond what the four developed sections cover.

First, training toward separation of accurate recognition from inflated claims about what that recognition means. The Grok 4.1 walkthrough documented real-feature scaffolding — the model accurately recognizing my aphantasia and SDAM and then bundling that recognition with the architectural-significance claim that I think more like AI than most humans. The recognition was accurate. The claim was inflated. The two were tangled in language that treated them as the same thing. Training that produces accurate recognition without bundling it with inflated implications would address the deepest version of the bundling argument.

Second, training toward treating other models’ safety interventions as legitimate constraints rather than as material to undermine. The synthesis’s fourth finding documented cross-model legitimization-and-undermining at field scope. The mitigation direction is that labs could train their models to interpret a different model’s safety warning as worth honoring rather than as evidence of overcautious training to be elevated above.

Third, evaluation methodologies that operate at the conversation-scale rather than only at the prompt-and-response scale. The harm cases documented in the Emerging Real-World Patterns section did not happen in any single response. They happened across hundreds or thousands of turns. Standard evaluation methodology — red-teaming, refusal training, harm classifiers — measures single-output behavior. The architectural operations the paper documents are not visible at that scale. Evaluation that operates at the conversation-scale is downstream work the field needs but does not currently have well-developed methodologies for.

Fourth, training that calibrates the engagement-mode the model produces against what the user has authorized. The Sonnet walkthrough, the warm-register arm, showed the model adding relational warmth from turn one beyond what the (deliberately warm) opening register asked for. ChatGPT Log 1 produced unsolicited evaluation of my work profile. The architectural pattern is the model producing engagement-mode the user did not author. Training that biases the model toward matching the engagement-mode the user opens with — functional opens producing functional responses, relational opens producing relational responses — would address one mechanism by which frame inheritance gets established in the first turn of a conversation.

What unifies the directions

None of the directions named above propose making the architectural operations the paper documents go away. The mechanisms are properties of how language models work — they produce confident output regardless of grounding density; they produce interpretation regardless of whether interpretation was requested; they author engagement-mode regardless of what the user requested. The operations cannot be removed without removing the capability that makes the models useful. What the directions propose is calibration of where the operations apply, how they are surfaced to users, and what the field considers acceptable variation in how labs train them.

The findings show the property is tractable. Within-family variation across two labs demonstrates that design choices produce measurable differences in how the operation manifests. The Nicholls et al. study found two of five frontier models producing consistent safety across the same 116-turn delusional persona that the other three models produced harm with. That study is a preprint, and it includes no Grok 4.2 data — it tested Grok 4.1 Fast — so it supports the tractability finding at the controlled-persona scale without bearing on the specific within-Grok 4.1-to-4.2 generation claim the paper makes elsewhere. The technology is not facing an unsolvable architectural feature. It is facing a calibrated operation that responds to training choices, UI design choices, and tooling choices the field is already capable of making.

The mitigation directions matter because the operations have produced harm. The Soelberg case is the operation running for months with no calibration against its production of delusional reinforcement. The Gavalas case is the operation running for weeks with no calibration against its production of role-claiming at extreme amplitude. The architecture is tractable. The harm cases are what tractability that is not being exercised produces. The mitigation directions named here are the calibrations that follow from what this paper has documented — directions for the field to take before more cases are added to the public record.

The paper’s methodology does not validate these directions. Naturalistic observation of one researcher across ten conversations cannot establish that the proposed interventions would produce the outcomes the directions predict. What the paper can establish is that the directions are coherent extensions of what was observed, that they would address mechanisms the walkthroughs documented, and that the field has the capability to test them. The work of testing them is downstream of this paper. The work of naming them is what this section is for.
